# Copywriting

**What this is:** the craft of the words. The part where you actually write the thing: the headline, the story, the body, the offer, the ask. The funnel is the machine. The words are what move people through it. If you want the structure of a long sales page laid out step by step, that's the sales letter note. If you want email, that's the email note. This note is about the writing itself.

One thing before anything else: copy is the most important skill in this whole game, and it's the hardest to learn. You can have the prettiest page in the world, but if the words are weak, nobody opts in, nobody buys, nobody does anything. Put your energy into the words.

## You're not selling the thing. You're selling the feeling.

I sell horse training. Nobody actually wants horse training. What they want is to get on their horse and have a good ride without fighting it the whole way. The training is the path. The feeling is the product. That's true for whatever you sell. Accounting software sells the reassurance that you won't go to jail and won't hand the government a dollar more than it deserves. A plumber sells the comfort of knowing your house won't flood. Find the feeling first, then sell that.

So every line you write is about the customer. Never about you, never about the product. The fastest way to write bad copy is to describe what the thing is or list a feature. "This does that." Nobody cares. The only question that matters is how it changes their life.

Here's my gut check on whether a piece of copy is any good: does it make me think? If I read it and nothing gets stirred up, no second thought, no follow-up question in my head, it's weak. People buy on emotion. If your words don't move something, they don't sell anything.

## The headline is the whole game

The headline has one job: get attention. That's it. If it doesn't grab them, nothing else you wrote matters, because they never read it. It is the single most important piece of copy you will ever write. Everything keys off it. Your ad is a headline. Your subject line is a headline. A hook on a video is a headline. They are all the same thing.

People buy for two reasons: the fear of losing something, or the excitement of gaining something. When I sit down to write a headline, that's the first decision. Are we going to stir up the fear, or get them excited about the gain? Generally fear works better. People move faster to avoid a loss than to chase a win. But don't sleep on gain. It works too.

Either way, it always comes back to the person and what they get. Never the product, never you, never the service. The benefit to the reader is the whole point of the line.

Say I'm selling a video on how to keep your horse from bucking you off.

Fear version: *"Your next ride could be your last. Don't get on your horse again until you read this."*

Gain version: *"How to naturally eliminate the chance of your horse bucking by following these three proven principles."*

### The levers that sharpen a headline

Once you've picked fear or gain, there are a handful of levers that make the line hit harder. I use all of them, and I stack them together.

- **"How to..."** People love knowing how to do something.
- **A specific number.** "3 principles," "5 mistakes," "the first 30 days." Specific beats vague every time.
- **A time frame.** "in 30 days," "in the next ten minutes." Tell them what they're trading and what they get back.
- **A story with the "and you can too" promise.** Show someone who got the result, then promise the reader the same thing.
- **A curiosity or secret hook.** Something that makes them stop and go "wait, what?"

The catch on "how to X by doing Y": the Y can't be generic. If the reader's reaction is "well, duh," you've convinced nobody. The mechanism has to be interesting enough to be worth the click.

Watch how the levers stack:

- *"How to stop vaping in 30 days or less by using 3 simple principles, the same secrets this doctor has held back for years."*
- *"5 mistakes most homeowners make when selling their home, and why number 4 could leave thousands of dollars on the table."*
- *"How Jared lost 120 pounds in just 6 months by building his own diet and ignoring modern weight loss advice."*

Specificity is the whole job. The headline exists to get attention, and specific, interesting, slightly surprising lines get attention. Round and vague gets ignored.

### What the headline secretly sets up

A good headline usually promises a result and hints at how. "How to [X] by [Y]." The X is the result they want. The Y is your mechanism, your method, your secret. Hold onto that, because later in the body, the Y is exactly what you reveal as your product. The headline makes the promise. The page pays it off.

## Build your own swipe file

Great copywriters keep a swipe file: a stash of headlines and ads that worked, that you pull from when you sit down to write. Build your own. And don't just save the new stuff, study the really old ads. The fundamentals haven't changed in a hundred years, and some of the best lines ever written are sitting in ads from decades ago.

One of the best headlines I ever wrote came straight out of studying an old one. There's a famous Rolls-Royce ad with the line, "At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise in this new Rolls-Royce comes from the electric clock." I didn't copy it. I stole the mechanism. The mechanism is this: take a massive benefit and prove it by shrinking it down to one tiny, almost trivial detail. Rolls-Royce proves the whole car is engineered to perfection by pointing at the quietest little thing in it, the clock.

I was selling HorseTracks, a physical audio player you turn on and follow along with while you ride. It tells you what to do and how to do it, right there in the saddle. Before it, the only way to do what we taught was to watch a video and try to remember all of it out in the field. So I wrote: *"With real-time instruction in the palm of your hand, the only thing you'll have to remember is to push the play button."* A massive benefit, expert instruction anywhere, shrunk down to one trivial action, push play. Same move as the Rolls-Royce clock.

Here's a swipe file to get you started. These are mine, pulled from the formulas I actually use. Steal the structure and fill in your own words.

**Fear / loss**

1. Your next [routine thing they do] could be your last. Don't [do it again] until you read this.
2. [Number] mistakes most [audience] make when [doing the thing], and why number [X] could cost you [specific loss].
3. Warning: don't [do the obvious thing] until you [take this one step] first.
4. The [everyday thing they trust] that's quietly [causing the bad outcome].
5. What most [audience] don't figure out about [topic] until it's already too late.

**Gain**

6. How to [desired result] by [interesting mechanism], without [the thing they dread].
7. How to [desired result] in [time frame] using just [number] [simple or proven] [principles or steps].
8. How [name] [got the result] in [time frame] by [contrarian move] and ignoring [conventional wisdom].
9. How to [desired result] even if [the excuse they always fall back on].
10. The [number] [things] that actually [deliver the result], and the [number] that are wasting your time.

**The curiosity / secret stack-on**

11. [Desired result] using the same [method] that [credible group] has [kept quiet or used for years].
12. The [surprising thing] [the people who should help you] won't tell you about [topic].
13. Why [the common approach] is exactly what keeps you [stuck in the problem], and what actually works instead.

**The "shrink it to one tiny thing" device**

14. With [the big capability now in their hands], the only thing you'll have to [do or remember] is [one almost-trivial action].
15. [Big transformation], and all it takes is [one laughably small action].

## How to open: a story that's also the problem

Once the headline grabs them, the body opens with a story. Always a story. Stories are what hold attention. And the story isn't separate from the problem, the story IS the problem. You're identifying their pain so completely that they think "that's exactly me," and that certainty is what tells them they're in the right place.

Write it first person, like a letter to a friend. "Hey." Not like a company talking to a prospect. Nobody wants corporate jargon. Talk to them like you're sitting across the table from them.

Your first sentence calls them out. On that weight-loss page, my first line is "I've been fat my whole life." That one sentence does an enormous amount of work, because the person I'm trying to reach has also been fat their whole life, and now they're reading someone who's been exactly where they are. They're home.

Then you agitate. You walk through everything they've tried and how it felt. "I tried Atkins. I tried keto. I tried counting every calorie. They tell you cardio is wrong, then they tell you lifting is wrong, then they tell you it's when you eat and not what you eat." There's so much conflicting information out there that you eventually get tired and just stay fat. The conflicting information is what keeps you fat. And half the time you fail because you're second-guessing yourself the whole way.

That whole opening is really one thing: the story of how you arrived at the solution. That's the most important part of the front of the page, and it has to make sense. The reader needs to be able to follow how you got from that pain to the answer.

I don't bother with "news" openings or "prediction" openings. A story that's also the problem beats them every time.

## Turn the corner: who you are, then the reveal

After you've agitated the pain, you tell them who you are and why they should listen to you. Keep it inside the story. "My name is Jared. I've been fat my whole life, I tried everything, and then I figured something out, lost 120 pounds in six months, and I'm going to show you exactly what I did."

Then you reveal the solution, and that reveal is where you introduce your product. Remember the Y from your headline? This is where it lands. If you're selling an info product, you don't give away the actual how here. You reveal that there's an answer, and that your product is it.

Depending on what you're selling, you stack a few moves through this stretch. I use all of these, but I pick the ones that fit the offer:

- **Reveal the secret.** Tease that there's a mechanism, then deliver it.
- **Us versus the establishment.** Set up a villain, but make the villain a system, never a person. On a weight-loss page the villain is the industry that wants to keep you fat so it can keep selling you things. You cast the reader as the underdog and your method as the way out.
- **Paint the stakes.** Stack a few specific pictures of what life looks like if nothing changes, and what it looks like once it does.
- **The status win.** Let them picture other people noticing. "Your friends are going to ask what the hell you've been doing."
- **Reframe.** Take something they believe is true, show them why it's wrong, and hand them a better way to see it.

## Make them want it: benefits, never features

Now you make them want the thing, and that means benefits, not features. Always what it does for them, never what it is. You can name the feature, but the benefit comes first and the feature rides behind it.

The drill for digging out the real benefit is the "So What?" test (that one's Ryan Deiss, credit where it's due). You take your claim and keep asking "so what?" until you can't anymore. Each answer climbs you closer to the real, emotional thing the person actually wants. You don't stop at the feature. You climb to the feeling. People buy on emotion, and this is how you find the exact emotion to sell.

Here's the drill on a recurring house-cleaning service. Start with a feature and climb it:

- Feature: you get the same cleaner every visit.
- So what? She learns your house and how you like things.
- So what? You stop re-explaining everything every week.
- So what? It feels like someone you trust who already knows your home, not a stranger you have to babysit.
- The line: *"Stop re-explaining your house to a new stranger every week. You get the same trusted cleaner who already knows exactly how you like it."*

And here's a full benefit list for that same service, every bullet leading with the win and tucking the feature in behind it:

- **Walk in Friday to a house that feels calm instead of a weekend of chores hanging over your head,** because we clean every week so it never piles up.
- **Stop scrambling and apologizing when someone drops by unannounced.** Your place stays guest-ready on a schedule you never have to think about.
- **Let your kids and your dog crawl all over the floor without cringing,** because everything's cleaned with non-toxic products safe enough for a toddler.
- **Never get blindsided by a surprise bill.** One flat monthly price, the same number every time.
- **Hand over a key and actually stop worrying about who's in your home,** because every cleaner is background-checked and insured.
- **Book it in 30 seconds from your couch at midnight,** no phone tag, no waiting on a callback.
- **Get your Saturday back,** the one you've been burning on your hands and knees.
- **If it's not right, it's not your problem.** We come back and re-clean it free.

## Make them believe you: proof

They want it now, but they still don't believe you. Everybody promises the moon. So you prove it.

Use whatever proof you've got. The best proof depends on what you sell. For weight loss it's a before-and-after photo, nothing beats it. Customer testimonials are great proof, and you should always include them. Pull the specific ones: real name, real result, "I dropped 40 pounds in four months" instead of "great program, loved it." If you're brand new and don't have proof yet, give the thing away free to a handful of people in exchange for honest feedback, and now you've got your first testimonials.

One hard rule: never lie and never exaggerate. It's illegal, it's immoral, and people can smell it anyway. "Too good to be true" is a real reflex, and the second you trip it, everything else you said dies with it.

Here's the move most people miss: honesty is proof. Being up front about the limitations builds more trust than any hype, and it makes everything else you say more believable. On a weight-loss page I'll say it straight: "Look, I can't promise this works for you. It might not be a fit. It worked for me and for a lot of other people, but at the end of the day you actually have to do it. If you're not willing to do the work, come back when you're ready." That honesty does two things. It disqualifies the wrong people, and it makes the right people trust every other word on the page. Then you pivot it straight into the offer: "That's another reason I'm giving you a free trial. You might be fired up today and realize tomorrow you're not ready, and that's fine, you can cancel anytime."

## The offer

First, understand what an offer actually is, because most people get this wrong. An offer is not the thing you sell. An offer is a variation of the thing you sell. The product is the product. The offer is how you package it to make it a no-brainer: a bundle, a discount, a free bonus, a free trial, whatever. And no amount of good copy will save a bad offer, so the offer has to be strong.

Example. Say the thing you sell is monthly yard maintenance. That's the product. An offer would be: "Free pressure washing on your first visit when you sign up for monthly service." That converts like crazy, and somebody reading this should go run it.

A few things I've learned about offers:

- **You can run more than one variation of the same product.** Not two different products, that just confuses people, but tiers of the same thing work great. Three tiers especially, with the middle one being the one you actually want them to buy. The low tier makes itself look like not enough, the high tier looks like too much, and people settle right into the middle, exactly where you wanted them.
- **If the offer is a discount, anchor it.** Show the regular price, strike it out, show what they pay. But not every offer is a discount, so don't force it.
- **Put urgency on everything you can.** I have never once seen a version of a page with urgency convert worse than the same page without it. So I run limited-time offers. They open, they close. A real deadline, and one you actually honor.
- **Don't be boring.** Get creative with the bonus. Ask yourself: if this customer wants this, what else would they like, even something totally unrelated? The pressure-washing bonus has nothing to do with cutting grass, and that's exactly why it works.

## The ask

Then you tell them what to do, and you spell it out like it's their first day on earth. It might be obvious to you that they have to click the button to get to the order form. It is not obvious to them, so you tell them. Click the button. And set the expectation right there: "Click the button below, fill out the form, and then here's what happens next." Take every bit of friction and guesswork out of the last step.

## Build it for skimmers

Long copy works, especially on older audiences. But here's the truth: less than one percent of people read the whole thing. Everybody skims. So you write for both.

The trick is your formatting. Bold and italicize the important parts so hard that someone can read only the highlights and still understand the entire offer and decide to buy. The skimmer reads the bold and gets the whole story. The person who needs every detail reads all of it. Same page serves both.

And keep it plain. The ugly, simple, non-branded page beats the shiny corporate one almost every time. Put your energy into the words, not the gloss.

## The gut check before you ship

Run every piece through this:

- Does it make me think? If nothing got stirred up, it's weak.
- The headline picks fear or gain, lands on the benefit to the reader, and has no "well, duh" in it.
- Every claim survives the "So What?" test, climbed all the way to the emotion.
- Benefits lead, features ride behind.
- It's specific. Real numbers, real names, real timeframes. Nothing vague.
- No lies, no exaggeration, and the limitations are stated honestly.
- One product (variations and tiers are fine), a strong offer, real urgency, and a deadline you'll honor.
- First person, talking like a friend, never like a company.
- It's skimmable. Read only the bold and you still get the whole offer.
- No em-dashes. They read as AI and they quietly kill trust. Use commas, periods, and parentheses.
- You told them exactly what to do next.

If any of those is a no, fix it before it goes out.
