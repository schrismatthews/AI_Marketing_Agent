# Facebook Ads

**What this is:** how I actually buy attention with ads. Everything in here is what I run on Facebook, because Facebook is where I run my ads. The principles carry to wherever you advertise though, Google, YouTube, TikTok, all of it, so take the thinking and apply it on your platform. The one thing to burn into your head before anything else: ads are not where you make the sale. Ads are where you buy the attention to get the click. (For the words in your ads, see the copywriting note. For the funnel the ads feed, see the fundamentals.)

## What ads are actually for

Ads are top of the funnel. Their job is to raise awareness that you exist and to get net-new people into your world, and there are two ways to do that: send them to a lead magnet to get on your list, or send them straight to a cheap tripwire offer. People will tell you ads are only good for the opt-in. That's wrong. I run ads to lead magnets and to tripwires both.

In fact, the whole business was built on a tripwire ad. Back when we sold DVDs, I asked my brother Carson what the ten things were that everyone with a horse should know. He told me, I filmed those ten videos, put them on a DVD, and sold it as a free-plus-shipping offer, $4.95. That was the tripwire. The second someone bought it, a video sales letter ran a one-click upsell to instant access to all our videos plus ongoing new content for $19.99 a month, and about 30% of buyers took it. It did well enough that I quit my job and went all in, and that DVD tripwire became the entire company. That was 2014, and the whole thing grew out of one ad pointed at a cheap, irresistible first offer.

And it still works exactly the same way today. I recently ran a tripwire ad for a free trial that handed people lifetime access to one of our courses, and it did extremely well. So don't pigeonhole ads as a lead-magnet tool. A great tripwire ad can build an empire.

What ties the DVD then to the free trial now is one idea: the ad's job isn't to make the real money, it's to turn a stranger into a buyer or a lead as cheaply as possible. The first sale, or the opt-in, was never where the money is. Once they're a buyer or they're on your list, you've got them, and the upsell, the membership, and the follow-up are where it all pays off. The ad built the pipeline. The funnel does the selling.

This is also why a broken funnel makes a good ad look bad. You can be getting cheap clicks and plenty of comments, which means the ad is doing its job, but if people land on the page and go nowhere, you make zero. So diagnose each piece on its own. Good cost per click and people clicking means the ad works. If they hit the page and stall, that's a page problem, not an ad problem. Find the actual broken piece instead of blaming the ad.

## The ad's one job: sell the click

When an ad sends people off the platform, its one job is to get the click. If it's a video ad, the job is to get the watch. That's the whole assignment.

There's a second, smaller job: the ad and the page it sends people to have to match. Same promise, same look, same offer. If someone reads your ad, clicks, and lands on a page that feels like a different thing, they get that flash of "wait, am I in the right place?" and they bounce. Read your own ad, click it, and look at the page. If they don't feel like the same thing, fix one of them.

## Your ad copy is already written

Your ad copy is really just a headline, and a hook is the same thing as a headline. Here's the part that saves you a ton of work: if you've already built the sales page you're sending people to, your ad copy is already done. You take the headline and the body copy off that page and you turn it into your ads. You work backwards from the page.

That's why you don't need some elaborate grid of hooks and audiences to plan ads. Building the page already forced you to figure out who your customer is and what hooks land. The work is done. Pull it off the page. And it's the same move as email, where your subject line is just the page's headline in a different outfit.

This is also exactly why I say copywriting is the most important skill there is. It feeds everything. The page, the ads, the emails, all of it comes out of the copy you wrote once. (The actual writing, leading with the benefit instead of the product, the "so what" test, all of that is in the copywriting note.)

## Temperature: match the message to how well they know you

Every ad is aimed at an audience that has some level of relationship with you, and your job is to match the message to it. I sort everyone into three buckets, and this is not an exact science:

- **Cold:** they have no idea who you are.
- **Warm:** they know who you are, maybe they've opted into your list, but they haven't really bought anything.
- **Hot:** they've bought from you. Opt-ins can blur the line between warm and hot, and that's fine, don't overthink it.

The thing to get right: cold does not mean sell. Cold means get their attention, give them something good, get them pixeled, and figure out who's actually interested. Warm means ask for a small step, an opt-in or something cheap. Hot is where you sell the real thing. The classic way people light money on fire is showing a big, expensive offer to cold traffic. A stranger doesn't trust you enough to hand you real money yet, no matter how good the deal is.

## Targeting: lookalikes, not interests

I've tested both interests and lookalikes for years. Interests used to work great. They're horrible for me now. They might still work for some people, but they don't work for me anymore.

Lookalikes, on the other hand, work better now than they used to, and all of my cold traffic comes from them. A lookalike is just like AI: the more you feed it, the better it gets. The bigger your list and the bigger your sample size, the better your lookalike audience.

So what do you do if you're starting out with no list to build a lookalike from? You go broad until you start getting people pixeled. And you do NOT run lead-magnet ads yet. With no audience built, you run content ads, because the whole point at that stage is to start filling your pixel. Pixel everyone who visits your site, opts in, or engages with you, because the moment they're pixeled they've graduated to warm, and now they can start getting your lead magnets.

Once you've got pixeled audiences, you've got raw material for lookalikes everywhere. People who visited your page. People who watched your videos. People who liked your page or engaged with your content. Facebook builds a lot of these audiences for you automatically. Build lookalikes off of them and your targeting keeps getting sharper.

## Video ads run on the platform, not off it

Video ads work a little differently, and the difference matters. You do not send video viewers to a page, and you shouldn't. Two reasons. First, far more people will watch a video right there in the feed than will click off to go watch it somewhere else. Second, and this is the big one, if they watch it on your landing page instead of on Facebook, you don't capture the video-view audience. You lose the pixel data.

And not every view is equal. Facebook will count a "thru-play" at around three seconds. That person didn't actually watch your video. So don't build audiences off thru-plays. Build them off percentage watched. I use 25% watched as a floor, and you can build several audiences by depth: someone who watched 85 to 100% of your video is a far hotter prospect than someone who bailed at 25%, and they'll convert much better when you offer them the next thing.

Your video follows the exact same rule as everything else: it is not about you and it is not about your product, it's about them and what they get. Help them, solve a problem, answer a question. Most company videos flop because they're about the company.

Here's the unlock hiding in all this: because a video ad doesn't need a destination page, you don't need the rest of your funnel built to get started. Content is the very first stage of the funnel. You can make a video, put it on Facebook, run ads to it, and start pixeling people and building audiences before you even have a website.

## Building and testing a campaign

When I build ads, I run three or four variations, and you really don't want more than that. Once you go past three or four, Facebook stops distributing the budget evenly, it gets messy, and you can't tell who actually won. Three or four is the sweet spot, and it'll hand you a clear winner.

Then you improve the winner one variable at a time. Take the winning ad and make a version with the same copy and a different image. Make another with the same image and different copy. See which one wins. Then do it again, and again, tweaking until you've got the best combination of image and copy you can get. I stop once I've got a combination I'm happy with. This is the same principle as finding your winning copy first and then testing images against it.

## Scaling winners: ramp slow

When an ad is winning, do not march in and crank the budget. If you jack the spend up on a winner, you'll kill its conversion rate on the spot. I don't raise a winning ad's budget by more than about 25% every two or three days, and I wouldn't go past 35%. The number isn't magic. The point is a slow ramp. Let it climb without shocking it.

## Judging an ad: ROAS against the whole funnel

Measure your ads against your own baseline. Run ads for a while and you learn what a click, a lead, and a sale normally cost you. Below baseline, the ad's doing well. Above it, it needs work. No baseline yet? Judge on ROI.

If an ad is just performing worse than your others, turn it off. Why keep paying more for a worse ad when another one is getting you sales for less?

Beyond that, watch your return on ad spend, and understand that the right number depends entirely on the campaign and the whole funnel behind it:

- **A lead-magnet campaign** won't show its return for a while, so be patient with it.
- **A tripwire ad** you watch closely. Don't spend $20 to make a $10 sale. If I'm selling a $10 thing, I want that sale for $5 to $7. But here's the nuance: a tripwire isn't built to make money, it's built to buy you a customer and a buyer on your list. So breaking even, or even losing a little up front, is completely fine IF most of those tripwire buyers go on to buy your $1,000 product. When that's true, who cares what the tripwire costs.

That's the real game, and it's the thing your competitors can't beat: whoever can spend the most to acquire a customer wins. A full funnel lets you outbid everyone, because you make more per customer downstream than they do. And don't get seduced by cheap clicks that never buy. Cheap traffic that doesn't convert is the most expensive traffic there is.
