# Email

**What this is:** email the way I actually run it. It's the highest-ROI channel there is, and the one almost nobody runs right, which means being even halfway good at it puts you ahead of most of your market. The whole thing sits on two buckets: the automated sequences that fire on a trigger, and the live broadcasts you send week to week. Everything below hangs off those two. (For the words themselves, see the copywriting note. For the funnel these emails move people through, see the fundamentals.)

## Own the list, because you actually own it

Email gets declared dead every couple of years and keeps right on being the highest-return thing I do. The real reason it wins isn't the open rate, it's ownership. Your list is an asset you own outright. Your social following is rented from a platform that can change the rules, throttle you, or shut you off tomorrow. So build a following everywhere you can, but always be converting it into a list you actually control. That's the one nobody can take from you.

## Two kinds of email: transaction and marketing

Before anything else, understand there are two completely different kinds of email, and mixing them up will cost you.

A **transaction email** is an account update, or the thing someone gets when they buy or sign up: a receipt, a login, the asset they requested. A **marketing email** is everything else, all your content and all your offers.

Here's the part nobody tells you: protect your transaction emails by sending them through a different service than your marketing. I use something like SendGrid for the transactional stuff and a marketing platform for the rest. Why bother? Because no matter how good you are, your marketing email will eventually pick up spam complaints and unsubscribes, and that drags your sender reputation down. You do not want that dragging down the receipts and login emails people absolutely have to receive. Keep them on separate rails so the marketing side can't poison the transactional side.

## The one rule everything runs on: the bank account

Think of your list as a bank account. Every time you send content and don't ask for anything, that's a deposit. Every time you ask for a sale, that's a withdrawal. If everything you ever send is a pitch, you drain the account, people unsubscribe, and they start to hate you. So you alternate. Deposits, then a withdrawal, then deposits again. Keep the balance positive and the list stays alive and profitable for years.

## Bucket 1: the automated sequences

These get built once and then run on their own when someone does something. Here are the ones I actually run, and the ones I'd tell you to build first.

**The welcome sequence.** Fires the moment someone joins or buys. Three or four emails. Welcome them, reassure them they're in the right place, and tell them exactly where to log in. The most important job: get them to use the one feature you know, from your own data, makes people stick the longest. For us there's a specific thing that, once someone uses it, they're hooked and they stay, so the welcome sequence points people straight at it. The last email is simple: "you've been with us a little while now, any questions, anything you need help with? I read every reply, just hit me back." That one builds the relationship, and because replies are great for deliverability, it quietly helps you too.

**Lead-magnet delivery plus a warm-up.** When someone opts in for your lead magnet (a PDF, a checklist, a discount, whatever it is), one email delivers the thing, and that's all that automation does. At the same time, you drop them onto a second automation: the warm-up. This is where you send your best stuff, the past content that got the most response, your best videos and posts and whatever else you've got, to get them excited that they're on your list. Deliver the thing they asked for, then immediately start earning the relationship. (I picked up this structure years ago from DigitalMarketer's "The Machine.")

**Abandoned cart.** Exactly what it sounds like. Someone starts an order and doesn't finish, so you email them. The one thing most people get wrong: link them directly back to where they left off, right onto the order form, not to your homepage. Make finishing effortless.

**Ascension.** This moves someone who already took one step into taking the next one. A tripwire buyer up to your core offer. A core offer buyer up to your profit maximizer. Even a lead-magnet subscriber who hasn't bought anything yet up to their first purchase, because the second they buy, they stop being a prospect and become a customer, and that changes everything. Ascension isn't only about email, and it isn't only about buying. The whole funnel is one long ascension: content, to lead magnet, to tripwire, to core offer, to profit maximizer, each step moving them to the next. (The funnel itself is in the fundamentals note.)

One rule on ascension, and it's a guideline, not a law: generally, don't ask someone to take a step before they've finished the one before it. Don't pitch your core offer to somebody who just grabbed a free lead magnet. Their natural next step is the tripwire, something cheap or a free trial. Don't pitch your profit maximizer to someone who hasn't bought your core offer yet. Now, will somebody occasionally buy a tripwire and jump straight to your most expensive thing? Sure, it happens. But don't build your sequences around the exception. Build on the foundation, and if your gut says test a shortcut, test it.

**Re-engagement.** When someone hasn't clicked anything in about 90 days, they trigger this. Three emails, about a week apart, then you wait one more week. If they still haven't clicked, they get a suppressed tag and come off the list, because I'm not going to keep paying to store people who never engage.

And notice I said clicked, not opened. That's deliberate, and it matters more every year. Clicks are real data. Opens are not, because Apple's privacy feature fakes opens, it pings the open whether the person actually opened the email or not. On our own re-engagement campaign we watched thousands of "opens" roll in with almost no clicks behind them. Those opens weren't real people, they were Apple. So build your triggers and your judgments on clicks. Clicks don't lie.

## Bucket 2: the broadcasts

These are the emails you send to the list week to week, and they all run on the bank account. Every broadcast is one of two things: I'm sending content, or I'm going for a sale. And here's a rule I hold to either way: there's always an opportunity to buy something in the email, even a content email. Even when I'm making a deposit, there's a link in there somewhere.

**How often to send.** Honest answer: it depends on your audience, and you have to test it. I wish I had a magic number for you and I don't. Some people, like Ben Settle (worth studying, he's got a product called Email Players), email every single day, a content email with a pitch at the bottom, and it works for him. Find what your list will bear. What I can tell you is the people who email more tend to make more.

**How I run an offer.** When we run an offer, we open it Wednesday and close it Sunday. There's nothing magic about those days except that Sunday is the end of the week, so closing then just makes sense. Across that stretch:

- **Wednesday (open):** a more content-heavy email, but it still asks for the sale. More content than the rest of the sequence, just not pure content.
- **The middle:** plain and in your face. "Here's the deal, here's the offer, go get it."
- **Sunday (close):** "This closes tonight. If you haven't grabbed it, now is the time."
- **Sunday, one more, to clickers only:** I take everyone who clicked any link all week and send them one final "this is about to close" reminder.

Then I give the list a breather, nothing Monday or Tuesday, and from Wednesday to the next Sunday I send two or three pure content emails to refill the account before the next ask.

**One email, one idea.** Keep each email to a single idea. This one is honestly more for you than for the reader, because forcing yourself to stay on one idea keeps you focused and makes you write a tighter, better email instead of wandering all over the place.

**Tie it to the offer in the P.S.** The way I link a content email to the product is usually through the P.S. The whole email centers on one thing, and the P.S. ties that one thing to what I sell. Say the content email is about how to fix a buddy sour horse. The P.S. says, "by the way, I've got a video showing exactly how to do this," and then I tell the quick story of how that video came about, the girl who showed up at the rodeo, whatever it was, and I drop the link to watch it on the membership site. The content earns the read, the P.S. makes the offer.

## Make it a story, not a memo

Your emails do not have to be boring, and the fix is stories. Tell a story in just about every email, even the ones that sell. Take whatever you're talking about and relate it to a story they already know, or a story from your own life. The only two emails that don't need a story are the bare "here's the offer, go buy it" email and the "it's closing tonight" email. Everything else, give them a story. People love them, and a story is what makes someone actually read all the way to the bottom.

## Where the content actually comes from

The blank page is the thing that stops most people, and it's solvable. If you genuinely can't think of what to send, you don't know your niche well enough yet. But practically, the content is all around you, and your AI can mine most of it for you:

- **Your existing content.** We sell information, so a lot of our emails come from the transcripts of our videos. Feed those to your AI and it'll pull content out of them and help you build a story around it.
- **Your own life.** Point your AI at your daily notes and it'll surface things that happened to you that are worth telling. Your stories don't even have to be about your product. Take something from your life and bridge it to what you sell through a story, and your AI can help you build that bridge.
- **Your customers' questions.** Keep a running log of every question customers ask you. You will never run out, and that log is a content goldmine.
- **Comments and social.** The comments on your posts and your ads are some of the best content fuel there is.

The thing that makes any of this work instead of turning into AI slop is context. The more your AI knows about you, your customer, and what you sell, the better its ideas get. That's the whole game, and it's covered in my core takes.

## The metrics I actually watch

Keep it simple. Watch your **clicks**, because clicks are the only real engagement data you've got. Watch your **complaints and deliverability**, because spam complaints wreck your ability to land in the inbox at all. That's basically it. Don't put much weight on open rate anymore. Apple made it close to meaningless.
