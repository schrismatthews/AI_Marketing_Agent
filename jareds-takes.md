# jaredrhod's marketing takes

How I actually think about marketing. Not the textbook version, the version I've run for real money across multiple businesses. Blunt, opinionated, and built to apply to whatever you sell. Grouped into the areas that matter: the foundations, copywriting, your offer, ads, email, and the AI workflow that runs all of it.

## The Foundations

### The product comes first.
Marketing can't save a bad product. If you make shit, it doesn't matter how good your marketing is. People might buy once, but they'll refund it, and if it's a subscription they'll cancel, because they don't like it. Marketing is an amplifier. Point it at something great and you win big. Point it at garbage and you just help more people find out it's garbage, faster. Great product with mediocre marketing still sells. Great product with great marketing is a no-brainer. Build something you'd stake your name on first. Then market the hell out of it.

### You're not selling the product. You're selling the feeling.
We sell horse training. But that is not what people are buying. What we actually sell is a good relationship with your horse. Nobody buys a horse because they want to train it. They buy a horse because they want the feeling of getting on, going for a ride, and having a good time without fighting their horse the whole way. The training is the path. The feeling is the product. So don't describe what you do. Put yourself in your customer's shoes and ask what they actually feel after they use the thing. Once you look for it, it's everywhere. Sell accounting software? You're selling reassurance: don't go to jail, and don't hand the government a dollar more than it deserves, which is zero. Fix sinks? You're selling the comfort of knowing your house won't flood. Sell roses? Most buyers are men buying for their wife or girlfriend. You're selling brownie points with women. Find the feeling, then sell that.

### You make your money after the first sale, not on it.
The hardest thing in business is getting a stranger to pull out a card the first time. So make the first time cheap and easy. Sell a tripwire and don't worry about profit on it. It's not that I'm willing to lose money up front. It's that I'm not worried about making it there. Someone who opens their wallet once, even for a dollar, is a completely different person than someone who only ever takes your free stuff. A buyer behaves like a buyer. Then right after they buy, make the real thing stupid easy to grab: a one-click upsell on the next page, card already on file, no forms again. Selling a $20/month subscription cold is brutal. Getting someone who just bought your $5 thing to click one button is not. As long as your tripwire-to-core conversion is solid, you make that front-end money back and a lot more over time.

### Give away your best stuff.
Give away really good stuff for free. It creates goodwill, and people want to return the favor. Frank Kern taught me this: give away your best stuff, and also sell the stuff you give away. Will some people take everything free and never pay you a dime? Yes. They're real, and they don't matter. The majority don't behave that way. Most people sell themselves way short on what they could make, because they're scared to actually help. They think if they give away the answer, nobody buys. It's the opposite. And the fear comes from not realizing how much you have. If the only free thing you can think to give is one tip, you're doing it wrong. There's more in you. Think about the one question everyone always asks you. There's never just one. That's your content. That's your lead magnet.

One caveat, because it trips people up. "Give away your best stuff" means your best free content and know-how, not your actual product. That part applies to everybody. The Frank Kern move of giving it away AND selling that same thing is specific to information products, where what you sell IS the knowledge. I can give away training videos for free and still sell a membership full of them, because information is infinite to copy. If you sell a physical product, software, or a service, you don't give the product away. You give away the content around it to earn trust and build your list, and you sell the real thing. Give away the content. Sell the product.

### Own your audience. Don't build on rented land.
Grow a following on Discord, YouTube, TikTok, Facebook, anywhere. Do it. But understand the catch: you don't own that audience. If something happens to your account, it's gone overnight. A list of emails or phone numbers is different. You own that. Nobody can take it. So build the following, but always be converting it into a list you actually control.

### Know exactly who you're talking to.
Defining your audience is one of the most important things you'll do. Different markets respond to different things. Older people still read their email. Younger people don't. Match the channel and the message to the actual human you're selling to. Try to sell a skateboard to a 95-year-old woman and she's never going to buy it. Sounds obvious, but most bad marketing is some version of exactly that.

### It's all learnable. Come in with a blank slate.
I learned everything I know from Dan Kennedy, Ryan Deiss, Frank Kern, Russell Brunson, and my friend Calvin, who to this day is one of the best marketers I've ever seen. I read their books, watched their videos, and sat next to Calvin and let him teach me. The thing that made it work: I didn't show up with a chip on my shoulder. I realized I didn't know anything, and I didn't argue with any of it. Blank slate. None of this is a gift. It's a skill, and the fundamentals haven't changed in decades.

### The way we market changes. The principles don't.
How we market changes constantly. Look at what you can do with AI right now. It's incredible. The channels change, the tools change, the tactics change. But the core principles of marketing work exactly as well today as they have since the beginning of time. People still buy on emotion. They still buy the feeling, not the product. They still respond to goodwill and to a real deadline. AI doesn't change any of that. It just lets you do all of it about 100 times faster. Point a powerful tool at solid fundamentals and you're dangerous. Point it at no fundamentals and you just make garbage faster. Learn the principles first. The tools will keep changing under you. The principles will keep paying.

## Copywriting

### Copy matters more than the page, and plain beats polished.
Copywriting is one of the hardest skills to learn, and it's the most important part of the whole process. People get caught up in how the page looks, the layout, everything sitting in the right spot. That's fine, but if your copy sucks, nobody opts in, buys, or does anything. And here's the part that trips people up: shiny, polished, corporate-looking pages almost always perform worse than a simple, plain, non-branded page. The ugly plain-Jane page wins, again and again. Put your energy into the words, not the gloss.

### Write long. Build it for skimmers.
Long sales pages work, especially on older audiences. I'd bet a million dollars that less than 1% of people read one all the way through. Everybody skims. So the whole trick to long-form is your bolding and italics: make the important parts stand out so hard that someone can skim only the highlights and still understand the entire offer and decide to buy. The other reason long works: the people who DO need every detail before they'll buy have it all right there. You serve the skimmer and the digger on the same page.

### Sell the benefit, never the product, and use the "So What?" test to find it.
You're always selling the benefit, never the product. So your headline calls out what they get and how they'll feel, not what the thing is. To drill down to the real benefit, use a question Ryan Deiss teaches: ask "so what?" and keep asking until you can't anymore. "Get my free horse training DVD." So what? It teaches you to solve your horse problems. So what? Then you actually enjoy the time you spend with your horse. So what? Then you enjoy your life more. Once you can't ask "so what?" again, you've hit the real benefit. Sell that.

### Good copy makes you think. Bad copy lists features.
Here's my gut check on whether a page is any good: does it make me think? If I read it and there's no second thought, no follow-up question, nothing stirred up in my head, it's weak. And the fastest way to write bad copy is to describe the product or name a feature. "This thing does this." Nobody cares. How does that feature improve their life? How does it get them what they want? It's always about the customer. Never you, never the product.

### Write like one friend talking to another, never like a company.
Nobody wants corporate jargon. "Company X has been in business for X years providing excellent, consistent, high-quality service." Nobody gives a damn about that. Talk to people like you're sitting across the table from them. Every sales page I write is first person and opens like a letter, "Hey" or "Dear so-and-so," like I'm talking to a friend, not a prospect I'm trying to close. Honestly, if more big companies wrote this way, they'd make a lot more money.

### The headline is the whole game. Its one job is to get attention.
Dan Kennedy said if you had a set amount of time to write a sales letter, you should spend 90% of it on the headline and the first paragraph. He's right. The headline sets the tone and the expectation for the entire offer. Its one and only job is to get attention. From there, the headline's job is to sell the first sentence, the first sentence sells the second, the second sells the third, all the way down. If the headline doesn't grab them, nothing else you wrote matters, because they never read it. This carries everywhere: your ad copy is a headline, your email subject line is a headline, a TikTok hook is a headline. A hook and a headline are the exact same thing.

## Offers & Trust

### Your front door stays open. Your special deals open and close.
The main thing you sell, your core product, should always have a wide, obvious, open door. Never make someone wait to buy the thing you actually sell. Special deals are different. A free lifetime course, a discount, a bundle, those open and close. A special deal with a real deadline converts. One that sits open forever doesn't. So I build a deal, open it, run it a few days, close it, run other deals, then rotate it back later with a fresh deadline. An offer opens and an offer closes. The core product never does.

### A generous guarantee makes you more money, not less.
People are inherently not assholes, most of them anyway. I give a long, generous guarantee because I'm confident in the product. Someone who's actually using it isn't going to ask for a refund, because they're getting so much out of it. Think about yourself: you're happy to pay for things that give you real value, and you don't ask for your money back on something that helped you and did what you expected. My expectation that they'll love it is sky-high, because it's a damn good product. And the guarantee puts their walls down and strips out their risk. "If you don't like it, I'll give you your money back. I'm not going to hold your money hostage, just tell me." That makes saying yes risk-free, and risk-free is what gets people off the fence.

### Address the elephant in the room. Transparency is one of the most powerful things you've got.
On every sales page I run a "What's In It For Me?" section, and all it does is tell the truth. Being transparent with your customers is one of the most powerful moves there is. The reader is already asking the question in their head: "this sounds like a really good deal, I could just buy this and cancel, so why are they doing this?" So you answer it. You name the elephant in the room instead of hiding from it. I literally say, "Look, we're giving you this as a bribe to get you to try our subscription. It's a shameless bribe, and it's a good one. If you don't like it, that sucks for us, we give your money back and you keep the course free." Then I tell them the real reason: we're so confident that once you try it you won't want to leave, and that's exactly what we're betting on. If there's an elephant in the room, highlight it. Don't pretend it isn't there.

### When the product is good, a subscription makes far more than a one-time sale.
It depends what you're selling, and some things just can't be subscriptions. But when it fits, recurring wins. For us it fits because of things like our AI feature: people use it every month, and it costs us money every time they do, so a subscription makes sense. We do sometimes sell individual courses, but those run $100 to $200 up front. A subscription is a bunch of smaller payments instead, usually with a trial, so people can sign up, take a few courses, ask the AI some questions, and see if they like it before they commit. And here's the math that matters: say we sold a course for $100. That's five months of membership. Our average customer stays at least ten months. So we make twice as much money selling that same person a subscription, and that's just the average.

## Ads

### Ads are for the top of the funnel, not the close.
What ads are best at is raising awareness that you exist, usually with content like videos, and generating leads with lead magnets. Can an ad sell a cheap tripwire? Sometimes. A core offer? Very rarely. But that's not what you point them at. So I run ads to put net-new people on my email list. The ad's whole job is the opt-in. On the page right after, I'll offer a tripwire, and some buy and some don't, and I don't sweat it, because the sale was never the point. The point was the list. Once they're on it, they get what they signed up for, then a warm-up sequence of my best stuff, all goodwill, and now they're mine to sell to for as long as they stay. Even if they never buy off that first page, they have unlimited chances down the road. The ad built a pipeline. The list does the selling.

### The ad copy is the headline, and the headline carries everywhere.
The picture you just split-test, but what matters more than the picture is the ad copy, and your ad copy is really your headline. That's another reason the headline is the most important thing you'll write: it sets the tone of the whole campaign. Nail the headline and it carries into your Facebook ads, any ads anywhere, your email subject lines, all of it. On TikTok everyone talks about "the hook." A hook and a headline are the exact same thing. Your TikTok hook is just your headline, or a variation of it.

### Find the winning copy first, then test images against it.
For ad images, you just split-test. Start with what you think will work, make a few variations, not too many, and find the one that performs best. Then take that winning copy and test it against different images and videos. Same process on the platforms where you use video instead of a static image. Copy first, image second.

### An ad doesn't "work" unless the whole funnel works. Diagnose each piece.
Getting ads to work means your whole funnel has to work, not just the ad. You can be getting tons of clicks, the ad is clearly doing its job, but if people hit the page and go no further, you make zero, and it doesn't matter what you spent. So examine each piece separately. If your cost per click is good and people are clicking and commenting, your ad is good, it got attention. If they then land on the page and stop, that's a page problem, not an ad problem. A broken funnel makes good ads look bad. Find the broken piece.

### The job of the ad is to sell the click, and the message has to match the page.
The one job of an ad, when it sends people off the platform, is to get the click. If you're running content like a video ad, the job is to get the watch. There's a second, smaller job: consistency. The message on the ad and the message on the page they land on cannot be different. Ask yourself, if someone reads this ad, clicks, and lands on this page, are they going to be confused and wonder, "am I in the right spot?" If the answer is yes, fix one or the other. The ad and the page have to match.

### Judge ads against your own baseline, or against ROI.
Measure your ads against how your past ads converted. Run ads for a while and you build a baseline: what it costs to get a click, an opt-in, a sale. Anything below that baseline, the ad is doing well. Anything above it, the ad needs work. If you don't have a baseline yet, the only thing to judge by is ROI. If it costs you $10 to get a lead and most of those people buy a $1,000 product, who cares about the $10. If you're selling a $5 product and it costs you $4 to get an opt-in, you've got a problem.

## Email

### If you run out of things to email about, you don't know your niche well enough.
If you can't think of what to send, that means you don't know enough about your niche, and you need to become an expert in what you do. You should always have something to talk about. Sometimes it's challenging, and that's where AI helps, as long as you give it context (we keep coming back to context for a reason). The more your AI understands about you, what you sell, and the benefit people get, the better its ideas will be. My agent writes all of my emails now and they're phenomenal, because it runs the same process as a sales page: it reads the customer avatar, the questions customers ask, all my copywriting notes, and then my knowledge base to learn my voice and everything I know. The difference between what people call AI slop and what's actually good is context. Give your AI context and it stops being slop.

### Your email list is a bank account. Content is a deposit, an offer is a withdrawal.
Every time you give them content and don't ask for a sale, that's a deposit. Every time you ask for a sale, that's a withdrawal. If everything you ever send is a pitch, you drain the account, and everyone unsubscribes and starts to hate you. So you alternate. Here's roughly what I do, but test your own, because every niche is different. One week I run an offer: open it Wednesday, close it Sunday, send three to four emails in between. One opener that's mostly content, one that's straight up "here's the offer," one that says "this is closing, act now." Then a final email a few hours before it closes, sent only to the people who clicked any of those links: "this is literally your last chance." The next week, no pitch at all. Three or four really good content emails, pure goodwill, refilling the account.

### The subject line is the email's headline. It's everything.
The subject line is 100% the most important part, exactly like the headline on a sales page. It's where you get their attention. Picture your own inbox: all the junk, the spam, the stuff you don't care about. Everybody's inbox looks like that, and you are not special. So ask what you can send that actually gets attention. Helpful works. Provocative works. Controversial works extremely well. And here's the biggest tip: if you're emailing about an offer, you already built the page, which means you already wrote the headline. Just use variations of that headline as your subject line. Your AI can spin those for you too, with the right context.

### Send timing matters, but less than you think.
We usually send at 9 in the morning or 2 in the afternoon, and that works best for our list, but you'll want to experiment and watch your open rates. Honestly, the bigger move is predictive sending, which some email tools do now (ActiveCampaign is one). Instead of blasting the whole list at once, it sends each person individually at the time they've opened your emails in the past. Everybody gets it at their own best moment.

## The AI Workflow

### Feed your AI deep context before it writes a word. Context is king.
This is the single biggest unlock with AI, and it goes way past copywriting. AI is only as good as what you feed it. Before my agent writes one word of copy, it reads three things: my copywriting material (books, courses, and articles from the best copywriters in the world, saved as files), my customer avatar, and the questions my customers actually ask. The avatar and the questions teach it who it's talking to, what they're afraid of, and how they really talk. Then it reads my knowledge base, hundreds of transcribed videos and podcasts. The difference in the output is night and day. That "the trail reveals spooky horses, it doesn't create them" headline that converted so well? My AI wrote that, not me, because it had all of that loaded first. Point AI at a blank brief and you get fast garbage. Point it at deep context and it writes like a pro.

### Build a "Jobs" system: one folder per job, with an index note that runs the steps.
Here's how I actually get my AI to do the work. In my Obsidian vault I have a folder called "Jobs," and inside it a folder for each job: "Creating a sales page," "Creating a Facebook ad," whatever the job is. Inside each job folder is one note named the exact same thing as the folder. That note is the index for the whole folder. It says what this is, what we're doing, then it lists the steps, maybe 8, maybe 10, maybe 12. Each step links to another note in that folder. For a sales page, step one is preparation: go read the customer avatar, the company knowledge base, the most common customer questions, and the copywriting files. All of it is wiki-linked right there, so the AI knows exactly where to go. It reads step one, goes and reads those notes, comes back and reads step two (write the draft, drop it in my inbox for review), and so on. By the final step the page is built and it hands you a link: "it's done, here you go."

### Consistency and organization are the whole game. AI runs on structure.
The key to making any of this work well is organization. AI is a computer program, and programs run really well on structure, and even better on consistent structure. So every folder in my vault has one note named the exact same thing as the folder, and that's the index. My AI knows that rule, so it always knows where to find the instructions. The whole vault has one master index note that gives the overview: what's in the vault, where to find things, and the rules (like "every folder has a same-named index note, read that one first"). The AI reads that master index when it loads in, and from there it knows how everything works. Consistency and organization are what turn a pile of notes into a workflow that actually runs.

### Memory on demand: load only the context the job needs, never the whole pile.
This is what separates my setup from just opening ChatGPT or Claude and typing. Those models have memory, but it gets compacted, the files are invisible so you can't see what it actually remembers, and you get drift, and the longer the session runs the worse it gets. I call my approach memory on demand. The AI only loads the context it needs for the job in front of it. Building a sales page? It reads the "create a sales page" note and the files that note points to. Nothing else. You're not bloating the AI with a bunch of context it doesn't need. And because the memory lives in the vault where I can see it, I can read it, fix it, and add to it. When there's something new to remember, you append the existing note instead of letting the AI spawn yet another memory file. When I first moved over, the AI had created 107 scattered memory files. Consolidated and organized in the vault, that became 17, more consistent and more thorough, and the AI finds what it needs instantly because the file is named the exact thing it's looking for.

### Build your knowledge base out of what you already have.
You probably already own the raw material for a great knowledge base, you just haven't turned it into one. Mine came from our content. All our videos are on Vimeo, so I used Replit to build a little app that hit the Vimeo API, found every video that wasn't transcribed, transcribed it, and let me download all of them as a zip of text files. (Most platforms auto-transcribe on upload now, so a lot were already done.) I ended up with 400-plus transcripts. This was before I had the vault set up, so I just fed them to regular Claude in batches, told it "I'm building an AI knowledge base, here are the transcripts from all our videos," and let it work. I had to do it in several passes because AI can only process so much at once, but even raw Claude doing multiple passes built something that worked great. It was absolutely worth it.

### Starting from zero: get the AI Memory Vault and hand it to Claude.
If you're starting from scratch and you want your AI to market the way mine does, don't reinvent it. I put my entire setup into a free repo called the AI Memory Vault. You download it, feed it to Claude, and it knows my whole system. It's basically a tutorial you give to Claude, and Claude walks you through building it, handling the work itself and only asking you to do something when it actually needs you. Get Claude Code installed, feed it that file, and let it go.

### No Obsidian or Claude Code? You can still do this.
Everything above is how I run it: an Obsidian vault, the files on disk, Claude Code reading and writing them. That is the full version. But the principle, load the right context before the AI does the work, does not need any of that. If you're using Claude on the web, you have two easy ways to get the same thing. One, turn your notes into a skill: a folder with a short instruction file that tells Claude which notes to read first, then upload it, and Claude pulls them on demand. Two, drop your notes into a Project's knowledge so they are always in reach. The vault is the most powerful setup, but it is not the price of entry. Get the context in front of the AI first, no matter which tool you are in.
