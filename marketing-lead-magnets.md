# Lead Magnets

**What this is:** the free thing you give away to turn a stranger into a lead. It's the top of your funnel: the ad gets the attention, the lead magnet gets the email. (For the opt-in page copy and the headline, see the copywriting note. For where this sits in the funnel and what comes after, see the fundamentals. For delivering it and the warm-up that follows, see the email note. For running ads to it, see the Facebook ads note.)

## What a lead magnet is, and what it's actually for

A lead magnet is something valuable you give away in exchange for someone's contact info. No money changes hands, but value does. It's a real trade: their email and their attention for your thing. That small yes is the foundation of every sale that comes after it, so treat it like the transaction it is. The currency is just permission instead of dollars.

Its one job is to grow your list with people who've told you they care about the problem you solve. That's it. It does not pay you back at the opt-in, and you shouldn't expect it to. It pays you back downstream, when that list moves through the rest of the funnel, the tripwire, the core offer, the recurring revenue, all of it. So don't judge a lead magnet on whether it made money the day someone grabbed it. Judge it on whether it built a list of the right people.

## Lead magnet or tripwire? It depends on what they give up

This is the distinction most people miss, and it matters because the two get treated differently in your funnel. The line is simple: what does the prospect actually give up?

- If all they give you is their email and their attention, it's a lead magnet.
- If they give you their time, or access to their wallet, it's a tripwire.

So a webinar is not a lead magnet. People hand you an hour or more of their time to sit through one, and their time is hugely valuable, so a webinar is a tripwire. And a free trial that asks for a credit card is not a lead magnet either. The second they hand over their card, that's a transaction, even if you never charge them, because you've got access to their wallet. A free trial with no credit card is a lead magnet. A free trial with a credit card is a tripwire. Keep them straight.

## Give away your best stuff

Frank Kern taught me this one: give away your best stuff, and if what you sell is information, sell that same stuff too. People hold back because they're scared that if they give away the answer, nobody will buy. It's the opposite. When you genuinely help someone for free, they want to return the favor, and that goodwill is a real force. Will some people take everything free and never pay you a dime? Sure. They're real, and they don't matter, because most people don't behave that way.

One scope note: "give it away and also sell it" is literal when what you sell is information, like a course or a membership. If you sell a physical product, software, or a service, the free thing is the content and the know-how around the product, not the product itself. Give away the content, sell the product.

And you have way more to give than you think. The one question everyone always asks you? That's your lead magnet. And there's never just one. There's a whole library in you once you start looking.

## What makes a lead magnet actually convert

These are guidelines, not hard rules. I've broken every one of them and still won. But this is what I'm actually weighing:

**1. Specificity. This is the whole game.** One problem, one idea, one segment, as specific as you can possibly make it. Here's the ladder: don't say "learn marketing." Say "learn copywriting." Better yet, "learn how to write headlines." Narrow it down until one person reads the title and instantly thinks "that's exactly me." The instinct to go broad so you don't leave anyone out is the exact instinct that kills your conversion. Nobody reads a broad promise and feels called out.

**2. Instant delivery and a good first impression.** A lead magnet by definition is: they opt in, they get it right away, delivered to their inbox the second they sign up. But the real bar is how they feel when they open it. It has to make them glad they handed over their email. If it's thin, or it's just a pitch to buy something, or it looks slapped together, they'll regret the trade, stop opening your emails, and unsubscribe. You made a bad first impression and poisoned everything after it. A good lead magnet actually does the thing it promised and solves the problem.

Now, the "they can consume it in five minutes" rule you'll hear is a guideline, not a law. One of my best lead magnets ever is a 30-Day Action Plan. You can read it in five minutes, but actually working through it takes 30 days, and it has one of the highest opt-in rates I've ever had. Fast to consume helps. Real value wins.

**3. Unique value.** Since anyone can ask Google or AI now, your lead magnet should carry something they can't get just anywhere: your take, your approach, the thing that's specific to you and how you do it. This one can be hard to pull off and it isn't strictly required, but try for it.

**4. High perceived value.** Free doesn't mean it should look free. The lead magnet is an audition for everything you'll ever sell them, so if the free thing feels premium, the paid thing feels even more premium. And this is the easy part now: hand AI your content, tell it to turn it into a clean PDF, and you've got a great-looking lead magnet without ever touching a design tool.

## The formats

Sorted by what they cost the prospect, using the rule above.

Real lead magnets (they only give you an email):

- **Checklist.** Easiest to build, easiest to consume, and often the highest converting because they can scan it in 30 seconds and feel like they got something.
- **Template.** A fill-in-the-blank starting point. Feels like you handed them weeks of skipped work.
- **A plan.** A specific, often dated plan they follow (my 30-Day Action Plan).
- **Case study.** A real example with a real name, real numbers, and a real timeline. Sells trust more than information.
- **Quiz or survey.** Works really well in the right niche, and it segments your list as a bonus, so every follow-up email can be tailored. The catch: quizzes are a pain in the ass to set up, so I only build one when the niche really calls for it. When it fits, it's worth it.
- **Sample.** A free taste of the real thing. The first chapter, the first module. They're tasting the product instead of reading about it.
- **Discount.** Email for a discount or free shipping. Not something I use at Carson James, but it's a legit lead magnet for ecommerce and anything with a clear price.
- **Free trial with no credit card.**

Not lead magnets, these are tripwires (covered above): a webinar (their time) and a free trial that requires a credit card (their wallet).

## Examples

Here are real ones of mine:

- **The Confidence Checklist** (a checklist). The headline clears 50% opt-in to cold traffic.
- **The 30-Day Action Plan** (a plan). Breaks the consume-it-fast rule and is still one of my top performers.
- **The Respect Test** (a quiz). Engages people and segments the list at the same time.

And the pattern in other niches, so you can see specificity doing the work:

- "Just got a new puppy? Here's the 30-day plan to a calm, house-trained dog." (one specific segment, one outcome, they self-identify instantly)
- "The 5 cold-email subject lines that booked us 7 figures in meetings." (a template, with a specific named result)
- "15% off your first order." (the ecommerce discount)

Now the weak versions, so you can feel the difference. "Subscribe to our newsletter" is not a lead magnet, it's a delivery mechanism, and nobody trades their email for the privilege of getting more email. Same with "sign up for free goodies," "get our updates," or a generic "free marketing guide." They all name a delivery mechanism and leave the reader to guess the outcome, and people won't do that work. Name the outcome for them.

## How to pick the topic: don't guess, read what they're already asking

The biggest mistake people make is brainstorming lead magnet ideas in a vacuum. They guess what their audience wants, build it, ship it, the conversions disappoint, and they blame the format. The format wasn't the problem. The topic was.

The fix is to read what your audience is already asking, in their own words, and build the thing they keep asking for. Your best sources, roughly in order:

- Your support inbox (pull the last 90 days, cluster by theme, the biggest cluster is your topic).
- Your community, group, or Discord.
- The comments on your best-performing content.
- Replies to your emails and your DMs.
- Competitor reviews, especially the 1- and 2-star ones, because they tell you exactly what people expected and didn't get, and that gap is your lead magnet.
- Reddit, Quora, and niche forums.

Cluster the questions, build the biggest cluster. The research is reading, not brainstorming. (This is the same customer-question mining that feeds your email content, covered in the email note.)

## The opt-in page

The page that gets the opt-in lives or dies on the same things every other page does: the headline is everything, plain beats polished, write like one person talking to another, and sell the benefit, not the thing (run it through the "so what" test). The full craft is in the copywriting note. The one job of the headline here is to let the reader self-identify in two seconds: "that's me, I need that." If they have to work to figure out whether it's for them, you've already lost them.

## My benchmarks

Measure the opt-in page itself, not the asset.

- **Under 25%:** there's work to do.
- **25 to 35%:** solid, a competent page doing its job.
- **Over 35%:** great. Sometimes that's the best you'll get, and that's completely fine.
- **50% and up:** exceptional, and it's reachable on a narrow, specific offer to the right traffic. My Confidence Checklist clears 50% to cold.

If you're under 25%, work the culprits in this order: a generic headline that doesn't let them self-identify in two seconds, a magnet that promises a whole category instead of one specific outcome, a cluttered page (too many form fields, too many CTAs), or just wrong traffic, where the visitors aren't actually in your target segment so even a perfect offer misses.

## The one thing to remember

Specificity wins. One big question, one specific segment, one named outcome, narrowed down until a single person reads the headline and thinks "that's me." That person opts in. The "everyone" you were trying to catch by going broad never does. And give away your best stuff, because the list you build with something genuinely valuable is worth ten times the list you build with filler.
